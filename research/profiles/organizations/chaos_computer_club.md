## History

### Founding (1981)

The Chaos Computer Club was founded in 1981 by Wau Holland and others in Berlin, Germany. It emerged from the burgeoning computer hobbyist and "hacker" culture of the early 1980s.

**Historical Context:**
- Pre-internet era (ARPANET existed but not public internet)
- Personal computers emerging (Commodore 64, Apple II, early IBM PC)
- BBS (Bulletin Board Systems) culture
- Cold War Germany (West Berlin location significant)

### Early Activities (1980s)

**BTX-Hack (1984):**
One of CCC's first major public actions involved hacking Germany's Bildschirmtext (BTX) videotex system.

- Demonstrated security vulnerabilities
- Transferred 135,000 Deutsche Mark from a Hamburg bank to CCC account
- Returned the money publicly to demonstrate the flaw
- Resulted in major media attention and establishment of "hacker = computer Robin Hood" image

**Cultural Impact:**
- 1983 film "War Games" introduced hackers as "smart but goofy teenagers"
- 1984 German media portrayed CCC as "computer Robin Hoods"
- Established reputation for exposing security flaws for public benefit

### Key Historical Incidents

#### Karl Koch / KGB Hack (1980s)

**Karl Koch (aka "hagbard"):**
- German hacker associated with CCC
- Sold information to KGB
- Found dead in 1989 (burned-out forest, controversial circumstances)
- Subject of multiple documentaries and books

**Note:** Koch's activities were not officially CCC-endorsed but associated with the broader German hacker scene.

#### GSM-Hack (1990s-2000s)

**Activities:**
- Analysis of GSM (mobile phone) encryption
- Demonstrated vulnerabilities in cellular networks
- Published research on encryption weaknesses
- Advocated for stronger privacy protections

#### Project Blinkenlights (2001-2002, 2003)

**Description:**
- Installation at Haus des Lehrers, Berlin
- Building facade turned into interactive computer display
- 8-bit style animations controlled by public
- Demonstrated creative/artistic applications of hacking culture

**Later Installations:**
- Toronto (2005)
- Sydney (2009)

#### Schäuble Fingerprints (2008)

**Action:**
- Published fingerprints of German Interior Minister Wolfgang Schäuble
- Protest against government biometric data collection
- Demonstrated that biometric data could be copied and distributed
- Part of broader anti-surveillance activism

#### Staatstrojaner Affair (2011)

**Background:**
- German government "State Trojan" (remote forensic software)
- CCC analyzed the software and found significant flaws

**CCC Findings:**
- Software violated constitutional rights
- Poor security allowed third-party access
- Broader surveillance than legally authorized

**Impact:**
- Constitutional court cases
- Changes to German surveillance law
- Demonstrated CCC's role as technical watchdog

#### Domscheit-Berg Affair (2011)

**Context:**
- Daniel Domscheit-Berg (former WikiLeaks spokesperson)
- Involved with CCC
- Controversies around data destruction
- Highlights complex relationships between hacker activism and whistleblowing

## Organizational Structure

### Chapters (Erfa-Kreise)

**Structure:**
- Decentralized organization with local chapters
- Called "Erfa-Kreise" (Erfahrungskreise = experience circles)
- 25+ regional hackerspaces in German-speaking world
- Each chapter operates semi-independently

**Notable Chapters:**
- CCC Berlin (original)
- CCC Munich
- CCC Hamburg
- CCC Frankfurt
- CCC Cologne
- Chapters in Austria and Switzerland

### Membership

**Current Size:**
- 7,700+ registered members
- Europe's largest hacker association
- Largest civil society organization dealing with technology security/privacy in German-speaking world

**Composition:**
- Diverse technical skill levels
- From hobbyists to professional security researchers
- Lawyers, journalists, activists alongside technical experts
- International membership (though German-language focused)

## Activities and Focus Areas

### Regular Events

**Chaos Communication Congress (C3):**
- Annual conference in Germany
- One of world's largest hacker conventions
- Thousands of attendees
- Technical talks, workshops, cultural events

**Chaos Communication Camp:**
- Outdoor camping conference (every 4 years)
- Large-scale temporary infrastructure
- Community building focus

**Gulaschprogrammiernacht (GPN):**
- Organized by CCC Karlsruhe
- Programming/hacking overnight event

### Publications and Outreach

**Datenschleuder:**
- CCC's print magazine ("Data Slingshot")
- Irregular publication on technology, security, politics

**Blogs and Online Presence:**
- Regular security advisories
- Political commentary on digital rights
- Technical research publications

### CryptoParty

**Movement:**
- CCC helped initiate CryptoParty movement
- Grassroots global initiative
- Free workshops on cryptography and privacy tools
- Decentralized, open to all

### Technical Advocacy

**Focus Areas:**
- **Surveillance critique:** Analysis and publicizing of government surveillance
- **Vulnerability disclosure:** Responsible disclosure of security flaws
- **Privacy protection:** Tools and education for citizens
- **Digital rights:** Legal and political advocacy
- **Whistleblower protection:** Infrastructure and support
- **Open data:** Government transparency advocacy

- German members may have CCC background

### Threat Assessment Context

**CCC Role in Ecosystem:**

**Positive/Defensive:**
- Exposes surveillance vulnerabilities
- Protects privacy rights
- Whistleblower support
- Security research for public benefit

**Potential Risks:**
- Knowledge dissemination (dual-use)
- Sophisticated members could be recruited
- Technical capabilities applicable to offensive operations
- Historical precedent for individual members engaging in illegal activities (Koch case)

**Overall Assessment:**
CCC represents Germany's legitimate, established hacker culture. Any connection to information terrorist group would be through:
- Individual members (not organizational)
- Knowledge/skills transfer (not organizational endorsement)
- Cultural context (German technical expertise pool)
